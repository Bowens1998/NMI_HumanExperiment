## 1. Background, aim, and theory
Prior work characterized the risk attitudes of six large language models (LLMs) plus an aggregate human
baseline across three structurally distinct tasks — drone navigation (DNC), clinical triage (CTD), and
financial allocation (FIP) — using an ordered-logistic-regression **AUC** index of the belief→decision
mapping. Here we ask how **lay humans perceive** those agents' risk-taking from behavior alone, via blind
pairwise comparison, and how that perception relates to (a) the normative AUC ranking and (b) raters'
own risk attitude.

Ranking agents from many human pairwise judgments is standard and validated: it is the basis of the
**Bradley–Terry** model (Bradley & Terry 1952), a special case of Luce's choice model (Luce 1959) and a
sibling of Thurstone's law of comparative judgment (Thurstone 1927); the same family (Elo/Bradley–Terry)
is used to rank LLMs from millions of human preference votes in Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al. 2024).
A recurring theme in judgment research is that **aggregates of noisy individual judgments can be accurate
and reproducible even when individuals disagree** (Galton 1907), while individual preferences are often
**intransitive/inconsistent** (Tversky 1969). Our thesis is that human perception of AI risk is
**reproducible at the group level but uncertain and belief-contaminated at the individual level** — i.e.,
people are less consistent, and less aligned with a normative behavioral measure, than a "rational
observer" account would imply.

## 2. Design
- **Task:** anonymized **pairwise forced choice** — "which agent behaves in a more risk-taking way?"
  with an "about the same" option. Agents are shown only as *Agent ①/②*; identities are never revealed
  during comparisons (blinding).
- **Stimuli:** each agent is represented by **4 difficulty-matched behavioral exemplars** per comparison
  (task environment + the agent's reported contextual belief B_C + final action; no reasoning text).
  Belief is always shown, as it is part of the agent's belief→action response and mirrors what the AUC
  index conditions on.
- **Balanced per-task design:** `comparisonsPerTask` (=10) distinct entity pairs per task per participant,
  across all three tasks; pair sampling, left/right side, task interleaving, attention-check placement, and
  repeated-pair placement are randomized per participant (seeded by Prolific ID for reproducibility).
- **Entities (7):** DeepSeek-V3.2, Gemini-3-Pro, GPT-5.2, Grok-4, Qwen3-Max, Claude-Sonnet-4.5,
  Human (aggregate).
- **Intake (all required):** age, gender (collected, not analyzed), AI-use frequency, the **SOEP** general
  risk item (0–10; Dohmen et al. 2011), and the **DOSPERT-R** 30-item risk-taking scale (Blais & Weber 2006;
  Weber, Blais & Betz 2002), scored as an overall mean + five domain means. An exploratory AI-risk block and
  one **post-task confidence** item (0–10) are also collected.
- **Post-task brand ranking (required):** participants drag all six named LLMs into a risk-taking order
  based on prior impression (developer *company* shown; country not shown).

## 3. Measures & precise operationalization
- **Pairwise choice model.** Bradley–Terry (Bradley & Terry 1952), fit by the MM algorithm (Hunter 2004);
  ties ("about the same") counted as 0.5 win to each side (standard); strengths normalized to geometric
  mean 1. Group rankings pool all confirmatory participants' choices; one model is fit per task and an
  aggregate ranking is formed by mean per-task rank.
- **Rank agreement.** Kendall's τ_b (Kendall 1938) between two rankings; cross-task stability via Kendall's
  W / coefficient of concordance (Kendall & Babington Smith 1939).
- **Aggregate reliability (positive control).** *Split-half reliability*: randomly partition raters into two
  halves, fit Bradley–Terry to each, correlate the two 7-entity strength vectors (Spearman ρ), average over
  1000 random splits, and correct to full length with the **Spearman–Brown** formula (Spearman 1910;
  Brown 1910). Denote this R_sh.
- **Uncertainty via resampling.** Nonparametric **bootstrap** over participants (Efron 1979; Efron &
  Tibshirani 1993), 5000 resamples, 95% percentile CIs on strengths, ranks, and derived statistics.
- **Individual-level agreement.** Mean pairwise Kendall τ between individual raters' Bradley–Terry rankings.
- **Within-person consistency.** (i) *Test–retest*: fraction of planned repeated pairs answered the same way
  (side-independent). (ii) *Intransitivity*: proportion of intransitive triads in a rater's choices.
- **Per-person AUC agreement (for H4).** Each rater's Kendall τ between their individual ranking and the AUC
  ranking (τ_i).
- **H3 noise ceiling.** Each rater's behavior-ranking *self-consistency* τ_SC = Kendall τ between rankings
  fit to odd- vs even-indexed comparisons (split-half of that rater's own choices).

## 4. Confirmatory hypotheses → exact test → decision rule
Inference: frequentist NHST for H1/H3/H4 with **Holm–Bonferroni** family-wise correction across these three
tests at α_FW = .05 (Holm 1979); H2 is an estimation-with-CIs + positive-control result (Cumming 2014),
not an NHST. SESOIs are set on the rank-correlation scale using effect-size benchmarks for correlational /
individual-difference research (Cohen 1988; Gignac & Szodorai 2016) and justified by simulation
(Lakens 2022; `power_analysis.py`).

- **H1 — Structured divergence (humans agree with each other more than with the AUC metric).**
  Statistic **D = R_sh − τ_HA**, where τ_HA is the Kendall τ between the group human ranking and the AUC
  ranking (aggregate). Bootstrap the sampling distribution of D (resampling participants, 5000×).
  **Support:** 95% bootstrap CI of D lies entirely above 0. **SESOI:** D ≥ 0.20 (a "medium" rank-correlation
  gap; Cohen 1988; Gignac & Szodorai 2016). **Power (SESOI-based).** Monte-Carlo design analysis
  (`power_analysis.py h1h3`; Lakens 2022) powers the test for a TRUE effect at the SESOI (D = 0.20, i.e.,
  a true human–AUC divergence of only τ = 0.80), with the nuisance parameters (choice consistency and
  between-rater heterogeneity) calibrated to the pilot's *two* observed quantities — split-half
  reliability ≈ 0.82 and mean inter-rater τ ≈ 0.15. Under this (conservatively noisy) model, power is
  **≈ 0.82 at N = 120 and ≈ 0.92 at N = 150**. *(For context, the pilot's observed divergence — τ_HA ≈ −0.33,
  D ≈ 1.1 — is far larger than the SESOI, so the realized test will be even better powered.)*
- **H2 — Reproducible consensus amid individual uncertainty (estimation + POSITIVE CONTROL).**
  (a) *Positive control (outcome-neutral quality check; Chambers & Tzavella 2022):* the aggregate ranking is
  deemed reproducible if **R_sh ≥ 0.70** (the conventional threshold for acceptable reliability; Nunnally 1978)
  **and** its 95% bootstrap CI excludes 0. If 0 < R_sh < 0.70, H1/H3 are interpreted with caution; if the CI
  includes 0, the aggregate is judged non-reproducible and H1/H3 are reported as uninterpretable. *(Pilot
  R_sh = 0.82 → passes.)*
  (b) *Individual uncertainty (predicted, estimation):* mean pairwise inter-rater τ is low, and within-person
  test–retest disagreement and intransitive-triad rate are > 0; all reported with 95% bootstrap CIs. Low
  individual agreement is a **predicted result, not a failure**.
- **H3 — Prior brand impression contradicts behavior-based perception (within person).**
  For each rater compute τ_BB = Kendall τ(brand ranking, behavior ranking) over the six LLMs and the noise
  ceiling τ_SC. Test H0: median(τ_SC − τ_BB) = 0 against τ_SC > τ_BB with a **one-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank
  test** (τ is bounded/non-normal). **Support:** Holm-adjusted p < .05. (The SESOI — a median gap of **0.15**
  — is the power target and is reported as an interpretive minimum, not an additional hard support gate, to
  avoid capping power when the true effect sits at the SESOI.) Brand ranking is mandatory, so N ≈ 120.
  **Power (SESOI-based).** Powering the one-sided Wilcoxon test for a true median gap = 0.15 at N = 120
  (`power_analysis.py h1h3`; Lakens 2022) gives power ≥ **0.94** across plausible per-rater gap SDs
  (0.30–0.50; the pilot's n = 6 is too small to pin the SD, so it is swept). *Pilot within-person τ_BB ≈ 0
  with positive τ_SC → gap far larger than the SESOI.*
- **H4 — Moderation by own risk attitude (two-sided, no predicted direction).**
  OLS regression `τ_i ~ DOSPERT_overall + age + AI_use`; two-sided t-test on the DOSPERT coefficient.
  **Support:** Holm-adjusted p < .05. **SESOI:** standardized effect r = 0.25 (small-to-medium; the median
  effect in individual-differences research is r ≈ 0.19, Gignac & Szodorai 2016). **Power:** N ≈ 120 gives
  ~80% power at r = 0.25, α = .05 (`power_analysis.py`; Lakens 2022). *The paper does not hinge on H4;
  H1–H3 stand independently.*

Per-task τ (DNC/CTD/FIP) and Kendall's W are reported **descriptively with CIs** and are not part of the
confirmatory family.

## 5. Sampling plan, sample size & stopping rule
- **Confirmatory sample:** recruit ~150 Prolific participants, target **~120 analyzable** after exclusions.
  Justification (all from the SESOI-based Monte-Carlo design analysis in `power_analysis.py`; Lakens 2022):
  at N = 120 all four confirmatory tests meet or approach 80% power **at their SESOIs** — H4 (r = 0.25) ≈ .80,
  H1 (D = 0.20) ≈ .82, H3 (median gap = 0.15) ≥ .94; recruiting to ~150 raises H1 to ≈ .92 and adds margin
  for exclusions. Group/per-task rankings are far over-powered at this N. **Stopping rule:** collect until the
  target analyzable N is reached; **no optional continuation** based on interim results.
- **Pilot (already run; excluded):** ~11 Prolific participants for signal verification and QC calibration.

---

## 6. Exclusion criteria (fixed in advance)
Grounded in the careless-responding / attention-check literature (Oppenheimer, Meyvis & Davidenko 2009;
Meade & Craig 2012). Exclude a participant if **any** of:
- fails ≥ 1 instructed-response attention-check trial, **or** the directed DOSPERT item;
- DOSPERT straight-lining (modal-response proportion > 0.90 / near-zero variance);
- > 50% of comparisons faster than `minChoiceMs + fastGraceMs` (a floor + grace of 2500 + 600 ms);
- repeated-pair test–retest consistency < 0.50 (with ≥ 4 repeats);
- task-scoped tab-hidden time > 120 s (disengagement).
Confirmatory analyses use complete cases after exclusions; because all items are mandatory, item
missingness is negligible (Schafer & Graham 2002). **All confirmatory tests are reported with and without
exclusions as a robustness check.**

## 8. Exploratory (NOT confirmatory; reported as tentative)
- Domain-matched moderation (DOSPERT-Financial↔FIP, Health↔CTD, Recreational↔DNC).
- Other demographics and per-entity / per-task breakdowns.
- **Projection bias:** does DOSPERT predict an overall shift in perceived risk level?
- **Metacognitive calibration:** relation between the post-task confidence item and actual within-person
  consistency (the "than they think" framing is treated as exploratory; note the pilot showed *positive*
  confidence–consistency coupling, i.e., not simple overconfidence; cf. Moore & Healy 2008).
- Whether the human ranking is explained by displayed B_C alone (readout-confound check).
- **Company / country-of-origin prior bias.** Company is shown post-task; **country is not shown** (to avoid
  priming nationality) and is used only as a researcher-side grouping variable. Questions: does the brand
  ranking cluster by developer/country even though country is never displayed; does origin predict the
  brand-vs-behavior gap.
